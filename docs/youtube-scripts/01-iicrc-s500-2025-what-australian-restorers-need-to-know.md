## Section 1: What is IICRC S500 and why it governs Australian water damage work (0:45–2:30)

**Key points:**
- IICRC S500 is the global standard for professional water damage restoration
- In Australia, it interacts with AS/NZS 3012:2019 (electrical safety on construction sites) and state WHS requirements
- The 2025 revision is the first major update since 2015 — 10 years of science, building science, and psychrometric research has been incorporated
- Australia adopted it faster than expected because IAG, Suncorp, and QBE assessors were already referencing the 2021 draft in tender documents
- If you're on a Steadfast panel or working through Crawford/Sedgwick — they're expecting S500:2025 language in your scopes

**Talking point: Australian context**
> "The thing that's different about Australia is our climate zones. The psychrometric targets in S500:2025 were written for North American climates — 21°C, 50% RH as the baseline. In Queensland and the Northern Territory, ambient conditions are genuinely different. The standard accommodates this, but you need to know where to cite it when an assessor questions your drying targets."

## Section 2: The three biggest changes in S500:2025 (2:30–6:00)

### Change 1: Damage Classification update (2:30–3:30)
- Class 4 (Specialty Drying — concrete, hardwood, plaster) has been expanded with clearer evaporation rate tables
- New psychrometric calculation requirements — you can no longer just say "Class 2, 5 days" — you need documented initial GPP readings and target GPP calculations
- **What to do:** Update your scopes to include initial GPP reading + target GPP + estimated drying days calculation

### Change 2: Category 2 water expanded definition (3:30–4:30)
- The 2025 revision clarifies that any water that has been in contact with contaminated surfaces (not just sewage) escalates to Category 2
- This matters enormously in Australia because of the prevalence of flat roofs, subfloor flooding, and grey water systems
- Insurers are now querying Category 1 assessments where subfloor exposure occurred
- **What to do:** Document source assessment methodology in your scopes — don't just state Category 1/2, state your process for determining it

### Change 3: Documentation requirements formalised (4:30–6:00)
- S500:2025 §12 (Documentation and Reporting) is now prescriptive, not advisory
- Must include: psychrometric readings at defined intervals, equipment placement diagram with square meterage, daily monitoring log, final clearance reading vs target comparison
- In Australia, this aligns with your WHS obligations for site documentation
- **What to do:** If you're hand-writing daily monitoring logs, you need to move to a digital system — not because S500 says "use software," but because the documentation standard is now auditable

## Section 3: What assessors are actually checking (6:00–8:30)

**Talking point: The assessor perspective**
> "I've spoken with assessors at [reference general industry conversation — don't name specific company]. The standard playbook now is: open the scope → find the psychrometric section → check whether Class × Category = correct equipment type → check whether equipment quantities match S500 §11 ratios → check for daily monitoring with readings. If any of those boxes aren't ticked, the file goes back. That's a delay. In a cash flow business, delays are existential."

**The three things assessors fail scopes on:**
1. Equipment quantities not justified against S500 §11 ratios (1 dehu per 40m², 1 air mover per 15m²)
2. No psychrometric readings cited — just narrative ("drying was completed successfully")
3. Category assessment not documented — just stated

**What a passing scope looks like:**
- S500:2025 §3.2 cited for category determination
- Initial GPP reading + target GPP + projected drying timeline
- Equipment table with quantities justified against S500 §11 ratios
- Daily monitoring log attached or referenced
- Clearance reading vs target comparison in sign-off section

## Section 4: Practical steps to update your business (8:30–11:00)

**Step 1: Get the standard** (8:30–9:00)
> "If you don't have S500:2025, go to iicrc.org and buy it. It's about $120 USD. That is the cheapest compliance investment you will make this year. Read sections 3, 6, 7, 11, and 12. That's the core of what assessors are checking."

**Step 2: Update your scope template** (9:00–10:00)
> "Pull out your current scope template — the one you use for water damage jobs. Go through it and ask: Does it have a category determination section with process documentation? Does it have a psychrometric section? Does it reference equipment quantities against S500 ratios? If the answer to any of those is no — that's your Monday morning job."

**Step 3: Train your technicians** (10:00–11:00)
> "The people writing the scopes are usually your most experienced technicians. They know what they're doing on site — but they might not know what goes into the document. S500:2025 creates a documentation gap between what gets done on site and what gets written in the report. Close that gap with a 2-hour team session going through the new requirements."
