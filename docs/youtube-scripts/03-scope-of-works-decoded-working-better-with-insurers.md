## Section 1: Why scopes get queried (0:50–3:30)

### The assessor's job
> "Most people reading your scope are Chartered Loss Adjusters (CLAs) or authorised representatives working to a review checklist. They are not restoration professionals. They are document reviewers. And in 2025, their checklist has S500:2025 as its backbone.
>
> Their incentive structure: process claims correctly, within their authority limit, without overpaying. They are not trying to underpay you — they are trying to process correctly. When your scope doesn't give them what they need to tick their boxes, the file goes into a query queue. That queue costs you time and cash flow."

### The four most common query triggers
1. **Equipment cost without quantity justification** — "12 air movers × 5 days" with no reference to why 12
2. **No documented psychrometric readings** — "drying completed successfully" with no data
3. **Category 2 declared without methodology** — stated, not substantiated
4. **Labour hours not broken down** — "30 hours labour" with no site-by-site or phase-by-phase breakdown

### The business cost
> "A scope that gets queried typically delays payment by 15–30 days. On a $20,000 loss, that's a line of credit issue. If you're running 15–20 jobs a month and 30% are getting queried, you are effectively funding your clients' insurance company's cash flow. That's the real cost of a poorly structured scope."

## Section 2: The 7-section scope structure that works (3:30–9:00)

> "Every scope I write — every scope I recommend — has seven sections. This matches S500:2025's documentation framework, which means every section answers a question the assessor is going to ask anyway."

### Section 1: Water Source and Loss Mechanism (3:30–4:15)
> "What happened. Where the water came from. What category it is and how you determined that.
>
> The key phrase assessors want to see: 'Category determined by visual assessment of source and site conditions per IICRC S500:2025 §3.2.' That one sentence tells the assessor: you know the standard, you applied it, it's documented. It moves the file."

### Section 2: Emergency Services and Initial Extraction (4:15–4:45)
> "What you did on Day 1. Make-safe procedures. Initial extraction volumes or method. Photographs referenced.
>
> This is also where you document Category 2 or 3 handling procedures. If you used PPE and containment — write it here. If you document it, it's defensible. If you don't, the assessor has to take your word for it."

### Section 3: Affected Materials Assessment (4:45–5:30)
> "What was affected, how far the water travelled, what materials are involved. This is the section that determines your cost more than any other — because it defines the scope of work.
>
> Australian builders use materials that your software's default settings might not know. Specify them explicitly: 'fibrous plaster ceiling, double-brick external wall, timber substrate under vinyl flooring.' Don't say 'walls and floor.' Say what type of walls and floor."

### Section 4: Psychrometric Conditions and Containment Setup (5:30–6:30)
> "Initial psychrometric readings, containment approach, and your target conditions.
>
> For S500:2025 compliance, include: initial dry bulb temperature, initial relative humidity, initial grain pressure (GPP) reading, target GPP based on material class, and projected drying timeline.
>
> If you can't do a GPP calculation, at minimum record the dry bulb and RH and reference the S500 §6 table for your region's ambient baseline."

### Section 5: Drying Equipment and Structural Drying Plan (6:30–7:30)
> "This is the section where most scopes fail. Equipment quantities need to be justified. The standard is IICRC S500 §11:
> - 1 dehumidifier per 40m² of affected floor area
> - 1 air mover per 15m² of affected floor area
> - 1 air scrubber per 100m² for Category 2 or 3
>
> Write it as: 'Affected area: 62m². Equipment deployed per IICRC S500:2025 §11: 2 × LGR dehumidifiers [ceil(62/40)], 5 × air movers [ceil(62/15)]. Equipment placed per attached diagram.'
>
> That's it. That sentence is what approves this section."

### Section 6: Daily Monitoring Protocol (7:30–8:15)
> "How often you took readings, where, and what you were monitoring for. Reference your attached monitoring log.
>
> S500:2025 §12 requires documented readings. The monitoring log is the documentation. If you have it, reference it. If you don't have it, build it — even if it's a table in the scope document itself."

### Section 7: Drying Validation and Sign-Off (8:15–9:00)
> "Final readings vs targets. Comparison of initial and final GPP or EMC. Clearance statement.
>
> The sentence: 'Final moisture readings achieved S500:2025 §11.4 EMC targets for [material type] at [Class]. Drying Goal achieved. Clearance issued [date].'
>
> That sentence closes the job. It tells the assessor: the work is done, the standard was met, the documentation is complete."

## Section 3: Writing for the assessor, not the site (9:00–12:00)

> "Here's the mindset shift that changes everything: you are not writing a scope to describe what you did. You are writing a scope to prove you did what the standard requires.
>
> That's a different document. A site description document focuses on chronology. A compliance document focuses on criteria."

### The language of compliance
**Instead of:** "Water damage was extensive and equipment was deployed across multiple rooms."
**Write:** "Moisture content readings of 68–92% were recorded across 62m² of Level 1 flooring (timber substrate). Equipment deployed per IICRC S500:2025 §11 ratios: 2 × LGR dehumidifiers, 5 × air movers. Containment established per S500:2025 §6.4."

**Instead of:** "Drying was completed successfully after 5 days."
**Write:** "Day 5 moisture content readings: 12–15% across all monitored locations. S500:2025 §11.4 EMC target for Class 2 timber: ≤15%. Drying Goal achieved. Final psychrometric reading: 21°C, 52% RH, 65 GPP (ambient). Clearance issued 2026-03-25."

### The equipment diagram requirement
> "Put a floor plan in your scope. It doesn't need to be architectural. A hand-drawn diagram with room dimensions and equipment placement is sufficient and often more useful than a blank description. It answers the assessor's question — 'where was the equipment?' — before they ask it."

## Section 4: Handling disputed scopes (12:00–15:00)

> "When an assessor queries your scope, the worst thing you can do is resubmit the same document with a covering note saying 'as discussed.' That tells the assessor nothing has changed and they should query it again."

### The three-step dispute response
1. **Identify the specific objection** — Call the assessor and ask: which line item or which section is in dispute? Get it in writing (email is fine).
2. **Respond to the standard, not the person** — Your response should reference S500:2025 or the relevant Australian standard. "Our equipment quantities are per IICRC S500:2025 §11. Attached are our initial moisture readings justifying the affected area calculation."
3. **Escalate with evidence, not argument** — If the assessor is applying the wrong standard or an outdated checklist, the escalation path is: provide the standard page number, provide your documentation, and ask for the specific section they believe you've failed to meet.

### When to involve your association
> "The Master Builders Association and IICRC both have dispute support for members. If you're getting systematic queries from a particular insurer or panel — where the same scope structure is getting queried every time — that's worth raising at industry level. That's not normal. That's a process problem on the insurer's side."

### The documentation that protects you
- **Photographs with timestamps** — Every zone, every day, before and after
- **Moisture readings with GPS coordinates or floor plan references** — Not just numbers, but where they were taken
- **Equipment hire receipts** — Not just that you had 5 air movers but serial numbers and hire dates
- **Communication log** — Date, time, who you spoke to, what was agreed

## Section 5: Building a better relationship with assessors (15:00–16:30)

> "This is the section most restorers skip because it sounds soft. It isn't. The assessors who process your files fastest are the ones who trust your scopes. Trust is built before the job, not during the dispute.
>
> Three things I do to build that trust:
>
> 1. Call before the large loss. 'I'm attending a [category] [class] loss at [address]. Estimated scope is $X. I wanted to give you an early heads-up. I'll have initial documentation to you within 24 hours of attending.' Assessors love this. It removes their surprise.
>
> 2. Send a Day 1 brief. After the initial extraction, send a 1-page summary: water source, category, affected area, equipment deployed, estimated timeline. Not a full scope — just enough for the assessor to open a file with confidence.
>
> 3. Deliver your full scope within 48 hours of job completion. The insurers with the shortest payment terms are the ones who reward fast, complete documentation. This is a competitive advantage."
