## Section 1: What "AI moisture mapping" actually means (0:50–3:00)

**Demystifying the term:**

There are three distinct things that get marketed as "AI moisture mapping":

1. **Digital logging tools** — Replace paper reading sheets with an app. Not actually AI. Just database.
2. **Spatial interpolation** — Takes your meter readings and draws moisture contour maps between reading points. Uses algorithms (some call this AI). Useful, mostly accurate, well-established.
3. **Predictive AI** — Claims to predict moisture migration paths, drying times, or hidden moisture locations. This is the frontier category — powerful when it works, dangerous when it doesn't.

**What's relevant for Australian restorers right now:**
- Category 1 (digital logging) is table stakes — everyone should be doing this
- Category 2 (spatial interpolation / contour mapping) is mature, reliable, recommended
- Category 3 (predictive AI) is real but needs significant site-specific data to be trustworthy

## Section 2: Where AI moisture mapping works well (3:00–7:00)

### 2.1 Large open-plan losses (3:00–4:00)
> "If you've got a large commercial or retail space — supermarket, warehouse, open-plan office — spatial interpolation is genuinely useful. You take readings at a grid pattern: every 2 metres, every structural element, entry/exit points. The AI draws contour lines between those readings and shows you where moisture concentration is highest.
>
> This helps you with equipment placement because you can see the gradient — you put air movers at the high-moisture boundary to drive evaporation across the gradient. Without the map, you're estimating based on experience. With the map, you have data to justify your placement to an assessor."

### 2.2 Complex multi-room layouts (4:00–5:00)
> "In a large residential job — say, a two-storey house with a burst supply line — moisture migrates through walls, under floors, into ceiling cavities. Tracking it manually across 15 rooms is genuinely difficult. AI mapping tools that let you import a floor plan, drop readings onto it, and see the spread as a heat map are useful here.
>
> The S500:2025 documentation requirements actually make this more valuable — because the map becomes part of your scope documentation. It shows your equipment placement is justified by data, not intuition."

### 2.3 Day-by-day drying progress tracking (5:00–7:00)
> "Where I've seen the most real value in Australian jobs is day-by-day comparison. You take the same reading points every morning. The AI compares today's readings to yesterday's. It shows you whether you're drying faster or slower than your S500 projections, which rooms are lagging, and whether you need to reposition equipment.
>
> This is also where you build your S500:2025 §12 daily monitoring log automatically. The readings are timestamped, geotagged, and stored. Your compliance documentation basically writes itself."

## Section 3: Where AI moisture mapping fails Australian sites (7:00–11:00)

### 3.1 Australian building materials differ (7:00–8:30)
> "Most AI moisture tools were trained on North American construction data. Timber frame with fibreglass insulation. Plasterboard with standard paper facing. The drying rates, moisture absorption coefficients, and equilibrium moisture content figures baked into the predictive algorithms are calibrated for those materials.
>
> Australian construction is different. We have a lot more brick veneer, more double-brick, more concrete slab-on-ground. We also have Hebel and other fibre cement products that don't appear in North American training data.
>
> The result: predictive drying time estimates from AI tools can be meaningfully wrong for Australian building types. I've seen predictions that are 30–40% optimistic for double-brick walls. That's a commercially significant error — it affects your daily rate, your equipment hire costs, and your scope cost."

### 3.2 Australian climate extremes (8:30–9:30)
> "The psychrometric targets baked into most AI moisture tools assume a temperate North American climate as the baseline. That's fine for Victoria and Tasmania. It is not fine for Darwin in the wet season, or Western Sydney in January.
>
> When ambient conditions are already above 70% relative humidity, the AI model's drying time estimates collapse. It can't dry to the target EMC when the ambient conditions are working against you.
>
> Some tools handle this well — they let you input ambient readings and adjust targets accordingly. Others don't. Know which one you're using before you trust the drying timeline to a client or assessor."

### 3.3 Hidden moisture behind linings (9:30–11:00)
> "This is the big one. AI moisture mapping can only work with data you give it. If moisture is behind a lining that you haven't penetrated with a probe, the AI has no information about it. And some AI tools will extrapolate — fill in the gaps with statistical estimates.
>
> The danger is a confident-looking map that shows a dry zone, where there's actually hidden moisture behind a wall. I've seen this on jobs where the initial non-destructive survey showed clean, the AI map showed dry, but there was a slow leak behind the cladding that caused a mould callback three months later.
>
> The rule: AI maps are only as good as the readings you put into them. Use them to display and document your data — not to replace your physical investigation."

## Section 4: The Australian standard you need to know (11:00–13:00)

**IICRC S500:2025 and moisture mapping documentation:**
> "S500:2025 §12 requires documented moisture readings with timestamps, locations, and equipment placement. AI moisture mapping tools that produce export reports — PDF or digital — are the cleanest way to satisfy this requirement.
>
> What the standard doesn't require is that you use any particular tool. It requires that you have the data, that it's referenced in your scope, and that it shows your drying programme was appropriate for the damage class and category.
>
> So if you're evaluating AI moisture mapping tools — the question isn't just 'does it make good maps?' The question is 'does it produce documentation that satisfies S500:2025 §12 and can be attached to or referenced in my scope of works?'"

**Minimum documentation checklist:**
1. Initial readings with timestamp + location (GPS or floor plan reference)
2. Equipment placement diagram referenced against moisture contour map
3. Daily monitoring readings at same locations (standard interval: 24 hours)
4. Comparison of final readings vs S500 §11.4 EMC targets for each material
5. Clearance certification with final moisture map attached
